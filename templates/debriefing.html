Debriefing

The purpose of this study was to examine how we learn about others and make decisions involving other people. Past research has shown that people rapidly form impressions of others; however, less is known about how we learn about others through experience, and how such learning influences social decisions. Additionally, little is known about how social contexts change the way we learn about and thus make decisions about others. To test these questions, you completed tasks in which you were able to learn about others, and were asked to make decisions involving others as well. You may have been placed in different social contexts, e.g. learned about members of the same or different social groups as your own, been placed in a socially stressful situation, or been given feedback heightening different social concerns. Alternatively, you may have been in a control condition in which these social contexts were absent or not highlighted.

This study relates to more general questions regarding how we form impressions of others and how we learn through trial and error.

Further reading: Hackel, L. M., Doll, B. B., and Amodio, D. M. (2015). Instrumental learning of traits versus rewards: Dissociable neural correlates and effects on choice. Nature Neuroscience, 18(9), 1233-1235.
